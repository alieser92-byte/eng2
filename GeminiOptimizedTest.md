**The Discovery of Penicillin**

Before the 20th century, even a small cut could lead to a deadly infection because doctors had no effective way to kill bacteria inside the human body. This changed in 1928 when Alexander Fleming, a Scottish scientist, made an accidental discovery. After returning from a vacation, Fleming noticed that mold had grown on one of his petri dishes containing Staphylococcus bacteria. He observed that the bacteria surrounding the mold had been destroyed.

Fleming identified the mold as *Penicillium notatum*. He realized that the mold produced a substance that could kill harmful bacteria. However, Fleming struggled to extract enough of this substance, which he named penicillin, to use as a medicine. It wasn't until a decade later that researchers at Oxford University, led by Howard Florey and Ernst Chain, developed a method to mass-produce the drug.

Penicillin was first used on a large scale during World War II, saving thousands of soldiers who would have otherwise died from infected wounds. This "miracle drug" marked the beginning of the age of antibiotics. Today, while some bacteria have developed resistance to it, penicillin remains one of the most important discoveries in medical history.

#### Read an Academic Passage (Questions 16-20)

**The Psychology of Memory**

Memory is the mental process of encoding, storing, and retrieving information. Psychologists generally divide memory into three main stages: sensory memory, short-term memory, and long-term memory. Each stage serves a distinct purpose in how we process the world around us.

Sensory memory is the earliest stage. It acts as a buffer for stimuli received through the five senses. This type of memory lasts only for a fraction of a second. For example, the brief image you see after closing your eyes is a form of sensory memory. If we pay attention to this information, it moves into short-term memory.

Short-term memory, often called "working memory," holds a small amount of information for about 20 to 30 seconds. Most people can hold approximately seven items in their short-term memory at once. To keep this information longer, we must use techniques like rehearsal, which involves repeating the information over and over.

Finally, information that is deemed important is transferred to long-term memory. This stage has a seemingly limitless capacity and can store data for years. However, long-term memory is not always permanent. Over time, memories can fade or be distorted by new information, a phenomenon known as interference. Understanding these stages helps educators develop better teaching methods to improve student retention.

"Today we're going to discuss 'The Bystander Effect.' This is a social psychological phenomenon where individuals are less likely to offer help to a victim when other people are present. The greater the number of bystanders, the less likely it is that any one of them will help.

#### Listen to a talk (Questions 15-16)

"In biology, we often talk about 'Symbiosis.' This is a close and long-term biological interaction between two different biological organisms. It can be mutualistic, communal, or parasitic.

Today, let's focus on mutualism, where both species benefit. A classic example is the relationship between the clownfish and the sea anemone. The sea anemone has stinging tentacles that protect the clownfish from predators. In return, the clownfish protects the anemone from butterflyfish, which eat anemones. The clownfish also provides nutrients to the anemone through its waste.

This is different from parasitism. In a parasitic relationship, one organism, the parasite, benefits at the expense of the other, the host. For example, a tick feeding on a dog. The tick gets food, but the dog can get sick. Understanding these relationships is key to understanding how ecosystems maintain balance."
